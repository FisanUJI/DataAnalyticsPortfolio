Introduction

Flooding in Nigeria has intensified since the year 2000 with the most serious threats concentrated in the southern part of Nigeria, along the banks of the River Niger . Heavy rains during the months of June, July and August result in extreme flooding of various rivers throughout Nigeria, particularly the River Niger, where the floods caused material damage and loss of life in Nigeria and other countries along the river's path.

According to authorities, the flooding follows the persistent rainfall, the release of water from Ladgo dam in Cameroon which caused the river Niger and Benue to overflow. Lokoja sits at the confluence of the Niger and Benue rivers. Widespread flooding has killed more than 500 people in Nigeria, left around 90,000 homes under water and blocked food and fuel supplies, two government ministries said on Friday, October 14, 200.

In this project, the aim was to identify flood-susceptible areas in order to find solutions for the assessment and management of floods and to determine which region deserves the most engagement in the development of risk diminution measures or mitigation procedures. This project develops a spatial multi-criteria decision production prototype for the evaluation of flooding susceptibility for the city of Lokoja in Nigeria

Study Area

Lokoja is a city in  Nigeria . It lies at the confluence of the  Niger  and  Benue  rivers and is the capital city of  Kogi State . Lokoja lies about 7.8023° North of the equator and 6.7333° E east of the Meridian. It is about 165 km Southwest of Abuja as the crow flies, and 390 km Northeast of Lagos by same measure. Lokoja has an  annual mean precipitation  of 1,167.1mm. The  mean monthly rainfall  is 82.4mm, and the months of high rainfall are; September (15.2mm), August (12.8mm), July (12.7mm), June (10.2mm), and May & October (9.5mm). The  most driest month  is December with average rainfall of 0.3mm, daily mean temperature of 25.8°C, and average high temperature of 32°C.

Methodology

The main aim of this project is to delineate the flood susceptibility map of Lokoja. This involves four main stages:

  • Data Acquisition and Preparation
  • Spatial Analysis - (flow direction and accumulation analysis, proximity analysis reclassification)
  • Weighted overlay analysis
  • Delineating susceptibility zones

Materials and Data Preparation

The data used for this analysis include:

  • Administrative data (boundary shapefile of Lokoja)
  • Precipitation dataset acquired from Global Climate Data Centre and
  • Digital elevation model (DEM) from the Shuttle Radar Topographic Mission (SRTM) obtained from USGS portal.

The precipitation data was clipped using the boundary shape file of the study area. The DEM data was filled to correct for errors, while a flow direction analysis was performed to determine the direction of flow of the terrain.

Flood generally flows in the direction from higher elevation to the regions of lower elevations. Inveriable, regions of lower elevation are likely to experience flood accumulation. To determine susceptibility, the digital elevation model of the study area was used to perform the slope analysis which tells the degree in rise and distance difference of the study area which accounts for 15% of the weighted sum.

Precipitation data accounts for 35% of the weighted sum analysis of the susceptibility mapping. To determine this, areas with large amount of rainfall experience flash flooding over area with less amount of rainfall as spatially provided by the world climate portal. The rail fall data was classified in to 5 classes of natural breaks from which units where assigned

Euclidean distance is calculated from the center of the source cell to the center of each of the surrounding cells. True Euclidean distance is calculated in each of the distance tools. To determine this, the stream network of the study area was calculated from the flow accumulation. This stream network used a 1% of the total value of the flow accumulation as threshold to delineate the stream network from which the Euclidean distance was calculated.

Results

Flood susceptibility map was produced by applying overlay weighted sum to the reclassified slope, precipitation and euclidean distance rasters. The study area was divided into five classes of flood susceptibility : very low risk, low risk, medium, high and very high risk. It can be seen that the low susceptible zones are mostly located in the North-East and South-East part of the study area. The western part of the study area were found to be more prone to flood.

Conclusion

The practical and unique contribution of this investigation is the generation of flood susceptibility map for the study area. This is a very useful tool for the decision makers in the city of Lokoja, Kogi State Authorities and the Federal Government of Nigeria to reduce human harm and infrastructure losses.

Flood is a major natural tragic phenomenon in Lokoja in the Koji State, while many measures are considered to mitigate it. Therefore, predicting future flood by producing flood susceptibility mapping is an important step for reducing flooding impacts for land and watershed sustainable developments.
